Most engineering initiatives don't fail at execution

Every initiative I’ve watched die looked the same in the post-mortem.

Not “we chose the wrong stack.” Not “the team underperformed.” It was always some version of: we didn’t really understand what we were building until halfway through.

The interesting part is that this is fixable. It just requires the one step most engineers skip, because it doesn’t feel like work.

The work that doesn’t look like work

Senior engineers spend more time on planning than juniors realize. That’s not a slogan, it’s a bias I’ve watched play out in every team I’ve worked with: the most reliable shippers are the ones who appear to be doing nothing for the first week of a project. They’re not. They’re doing the part that determines whether the next three months go well.

The framework below is what that “nothing” actually looks like. Four steps, each with a concrete output, plus the thing nobody talks about.

Step 1: Do your homework

Output: A contract of facts.

Start from the objective and walk backwards. What needs to be added, changed, or removed to get from here to there? Most initiatives skip this part because the team has a rough vibe of what needs doing. But vibes don’t survive contact with the codebase.

Concretely:

  • Talk to customers. Not “what do you want”, but “show me what you do today.” Insights live in the friction, not in the requests.
  • Look at the metrics. Whatever you’re about to change, you need its baseline. Otherwise you can’t tell if you made it better.
  • Read the existing code. Not skim. Read. Look for patterns you can re-use, places that need a refactor first, and the reasons something is the way it is.
  • Survey competitors. They’ve already failed at the things you’re about to discover. Cheap way to learn.

Then ask the harder questions. Can the change ship in a way that’s compatible with existing patterns? A change that requires every consumer to migrate is not a change. It’s a project. Will it break customers? If yes, what does the deprecation path look like? Is everything you’re proposing actually a must-have, or are some of these “wouldn’t it be nice”? The MVP question is unsexy and unavoidable.

The output of this step is a contract of facts. Not opinions. Facts you can point at when someone says “but couldn’t we just…” three weeks in.

Step 2: Plan the work

Output: Objectives.

You now have facts. Turn them into milestones. Real ones. Not “Q3: ship the thing” but “by date X, capability Y is in production behind a feature flag, behind a percentage rollout, with metric Z reporting.”

Three rules I keep relearning:

  1. Tasks need to fit in someone’s head. If a ticket needs a paragraph of context to even understand, it’s not a ticket. It’s a project. Break it down further.
  2. Acceptance criteria belong on the ticket. Not in someone’s head. Not in a Slack thread. On the ticket. The person doing the work shouldn’t have to rediscover what “done” means.
  3. Plan for failure explicitly. What can go wrong? What’s the mitigation? The exercise isn’t about being pessimistic. It’s about not having to invent a contingency at 3am when something does break.

The output is a set of objectives the team can actually argue about, because they’re concrete enough to disagree on.

Step 3: Bring it to the team

Output: A plan that’s tangible enough to execute.

This is where most ICs feel the urge to skip ahead and just start coding. Don’t. The team discussion isn’t a formality. It’s the part where your homework either survives contact with reality, or doesn’t.

The right posture going in: be prepared to be challenged on anything. Including the parts you thought were obvious. The team will see things you missed. They will also be the ones who have to live with the plan.

Engage them as participants, not as an audience. Initiatives planned in a vacuum and presented as fait accompli get sandbagged in slow-motion later. The team that helped shape the plan has a reason to want it to work.

End the meeting with a consensus on scope: not on every detail, but on the boundaries. What’s in, what’s out, what’s “later.”

Step 4: Execute

Output: Shipped software.

The execution loop is unglamorous and well-known:

  1. Write the test case.
  2. Make it pass.
  3. Write the documentation.
  4. Refactor.

The point isn’t TDD-evangelism. It’s that the discipline keeps you honest about what “done” means at the unit level, the way Step 2 kept you honest at the milestone level.

What’s harder, and what people don’t write enough about: execution is mostly communication.

A weekly summary, sent on the same day each week, with three sections: highlights, lowlights, next steps. That’s the whole format. It’s boring. It works because it forces you to face reality every Friday and to make that reality visible to the people who are betting on you to deliver.

Transparency has a cost: it means showing the bumps. The alternative is worse. The people who depend on your work find out about problems too late to help.

The thing nobody talks about

The four steps look linear in a diagram. They are not. You will be in step 4 and discover something that sends you back to step 1. That’s not failure. That’s the system working. The original homework was based on what you knew then; what you know now is more.

The discipline isn’t to follow the steps perfectly. It’s to name what step you’re in when something breaks, so you know whether you’re addressing the actual problem or just patching a symptom further downstream.

What separates seniors from juniors

Not the coding. Anyone can write code. The thing that compounds with experience is the willingness to invest in the early steps when the pressure is to skip them.

There is always a stakeholder asking why you haven’t started yet. There’s always a calendar that says you should be coding by now. The senior engineer’s job, the actual job, is to know when “let’s just start” is going to cost the team a multiple of what you’d save by doing the homework.

Skip step 1 once and you’ll feel the pain in step 4. Skip it consistently and you’ll be the engineer who’s always shipping late and isn’t sure why.
